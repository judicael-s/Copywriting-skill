# Drayton Bird and Eddie Shleyner principles

Use this reference as the quality layer for the copywriting suite.

## Core idea

Good copy is not decoration. It changes what a specific reader believes, feels, or does.

Drayton brings the selling discipline. Eddie brings the lesson, rhythm, and human detail.

Together:

```text
one real reader
clear offer
specific proof
small human detail
simple words
sharp ending
```

## Drayton Bird operating principles

### Write to one real prospect

Do not write to a vague audience. Picture one buyer with fears, habits, objections, language, and desire.

Ask:

- Who exactly is this for?
- What do they already want?
- What are they afraid of?
- What do they believe already?
- What would make them stop reading?
- What ultimate benefit are we offering?

Useful prompt:

```text
To whom are we offering what ultimate benefit?
```

### Do the complete selling job

Do not only make a claim. Sell it.

Checklist:

- claim
- proof
- reason why
- mechanism
- benefit
- objection handling
- example
- next action

Short copy is fine when the buying decision is simple. Long copy is fine when the objections are real.

### Match the tone to the person and context

The same voice does not work everywhere.

- Outreach email: calm, specific, one wedge
- LinkedIn post: useful, reflective, story-led
- Landing page: clear, credible, action-oriented
- CV or application: proof first, scanner-friendly
- French copy: native rhythm, not translated English

### Research creates the hook

The best hook is often not in the product brief.

Look in:

- reviews
- objections
- support tickets
- sales calls
- DMs
- comments
- founder stories
- moments of use

Ask:

```text
What do customers say that the company does not say?
```

### Relevant surprise beats cleverness

A good surprise makes the reader think: that is true.

Bad surprise is quirky. Good surprise reveals a hidden consequence, tension, or emotional truth.

### Appeal to deep emotions

Clear copy can still carry emotion.

Use concrete details that trigger:

- relief
- pride
- fear of waste
- security
- embarrassment
- status
- hope
- frustration

### Repeat the main benefit with variation

Repeat the central promise through different forms:

- headline
- example
- proof
- CTA
- final line

Never copy-paste the same claim. Make it deepen.

## Eddie Shleyner operating principles

### Micro-lesson structure

Best for LinkedIn, newsletters, short essays, and social posts.

```text
small scene or observation
copywriting lesson
why it works
practical application
short close
```

The lesson should feel discovered, not announced.

### Story first, lesson second

Do not open with the textbook principle if a small scene can carry it.

Good sources:

- a line from a label
- a failed draft
- a client moment, anonymized
- a store shelf
- a strange ad
- a conversation
- a product detail

### Proof early

Trust is a narrow gate. Bring proof near the beginning.

Proof can be:

- specific result
- before/after
- testimonial
- demonstration
- named mechanism
- screenshot
- concrete detail

### Vivid word choice

One precise word can do more than a paragraph.

Prefer words that create:

- image
- friction
- consequence
- movement
- stakes

### Qualify big promises

A strong promise becomes more believable when it has a reasonable condition.

Pattern:

```text
If [reasonable condition], then [strong promise].
```

Example:

```text
If your Shopify store already gets traffic, a clearer product page can usually recover sales you are losing now.
```

### Copyworking

Study strong copy slowly.

1. Transcribe it by hand.
2. Mark the hook, turn, proof, and close.
3. Identify the structure.
4. Write a new piece with the structure, not the language.

## Quality pass

Before delivery, ask:

- Does the opening earn attention?
- Is the reader specific?
- Is the offer clear?
- Is there proof early?
- Is the copy doing a complete selling job?
- Could a human say this aloud?
- Is the final line inevitable from the first line?
