# Hook discipline

Use this when copy feels generic, flat, or polished but forgettable.

## Rule

If the opening is wrong, do not polish the draft. Change the entry point.

A better hook is not louder. It is more inevitable for the product, reader, and moment.

## Choose the entry point by situation

| Situation | Strong entry points |
|---|---|
| Product page | moment of use, buyer tension, objection, visible product detail |
| Landing page | pain, promise, proof, mechanism, cost of inaction |
| Outreach | public observation, missed opportunity, small specific idea |
| LinkedIn post | small scene, useful contradiction, lesson from work, proof-backed observation |
| Job application | role object, recruiter need, proof of fit, personal operating belief |
| Ad | image, ritual, friction, desire, consequence |

## Hook diagnostics

A weak hook usually:

- restates the topic
- starts too broad
- uses a generic question
- performs insight with phrases like “here is why”
- sounds clever but does not connect to the offer
- could fit any brand in the category

A strong hook usually:

- names a specific pain or desire
- creates a clear image
- shows stakes fast
- suggests a hidden mechanism
- carries proof or tension
- makes the reader feel seen

## Rewrite workflow

1. Name the job of the piece.
2. Name the reader’s current belief.
3. Name the belief that must replace it.
4. List 5 possible entry points.
5. Pick the one with the most tension and least explanation.
6. Rewrite the first 3 lines only.
7. Check whether the middle now has a stronger direction.

## If the draft still feels generic

Do not add adjectives.

Change one of these:

- reader
- promise
- proof
- image
- objection
- moment of use
- first sentence

## Useful prompt

```text
Give me 8 different entry points for this piece: pain, proof, image, objection, story, contrarian, mechanism, and direct benefit. Then pick the strongest and explain why.
```
